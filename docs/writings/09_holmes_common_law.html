Legal Judgment as Historical Accretion (Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.)

Overview

Holmes presents legal judgment as a historically accumulated capacity rather than deductive rule application. “The life of the law has not been logic; it has been experience.”

Judgment precedes rules. Rules summarize it.

Law as Experience

Legal doctrines emerge from repeated encounters with cases. Rules are abstractions extracted afterward.

Judgment cannot be reduced to formal logic.

Precedent as Continuity

Precedent preserves trajectory, not formula. Judges situate cases within evolving lines of decision.

Judgment consists in navigating continuity under change.

Institutional Memory

Legal judgment is collective. Courts preserve continuity across generations.

This memory is not a database but a shared sensibility.

Irreversibility

Legal development is path-dependent. Each judgment carries the weight of prior ones.

Judgment moves forward by accretion.

Structural Implication

Judgment cannot be preserved by codification alone. It requires preservation of historical continuity.

Systems that record outcomes while erasing sequence preserve law while losing judgment.
