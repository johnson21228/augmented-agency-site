Judgment as a Scarce, Time-Formed Resource (David Autor)

Overview

David Autor’s work on labor economics distinguishes tasks from judgment. Tasks can often be automated; judgment resists codification because it is time-formed and context-dependent.

Automation displaces tasks, not judgment—but it often erodes the conditions judgment requires.

Tasks Versus Judgment

Tasks are specifiable units of work. Judgment integrates, prioritizes, and adapts tasks under uncertainty.

Judgment appears where rules end.

Codifiability and Time

Tasks become automatable when knowledge can be written down.

Judgment cannot, because it is formed through exposure, error, and feedback over time.

Displacement Without Support

Automation often removes the scaffolding through which judgment was exercised.

Demand for judgment rises while opportunities to form it shrink.

Polarization and Continuity Loss

Middle-skill roles historically supported judgment formation.

Their erosion fragments experience and undermines continuity.

Complementarity Without Continuity

Human-machine complementarity does not preserve judgment unless continuity is maintained.

Intermittent oversight degrades judgment.

Structural Implication

Judgment is a form of capital that accumulates slowly and decays when unused.

Systems that optimize tasks while fragmenting experience preserve efficiency while eroding agency.
