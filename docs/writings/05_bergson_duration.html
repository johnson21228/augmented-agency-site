Duration and the Irreversibility of Judgment (Henri Bergson)

Overview

Henri Bergson’s philosophy centers on the distinction between lived time (durée) and spatialized time. In Time and Free Will and later works, Bergson argues that treating time as a sequence of discrete units distorts cognition at its core. Judgment, on his account, depends on duration: a qualitative continuity in which past, present, and future interpenetrate.

Judgment cannot survive the reduction of time to measurable instants.

Duration Versus Spatialized Time

Bergson rejects the conception of time as a line of identical moments. This conception, while useful for measurement, imports spatial reasoning into temporal experience.

Duration is lived time. Moments are not juxtaposed; they permeate one another. The present is thick with the past, and the future is already nascent in the direction of the flow.

Judgment occurs only within this continuity.

Judgment Against Choice Among States

Bergson distinguishes genuine judgment from the selection among pre-existing options. The latter presupposes that possibilities are fully formed in advance.

In lived experience, judgment is creative. The situation does not present a fixed menu. The response emerges as the situation unfolds, shaped by accumulated experience.

Judgment is formation, not selection.

Accumulation of the Past

In duration, the past is preserved not as stored images but as pressure and orientation. Each moment carries the entire past in transformed form.

Judgment depends on this accumulation. What is perceived as relevant or urgent depends on how the past has been integrated into the present.

No amount of information can substitute for this continuity.

Irreversibility and Freedom

Duration is irreversible. Once lived, it cannot be replayed. This irreversibility grounds freedom: action is neither mechanically determined nor arbitrary.

Judgment expresses the entire history of the agent at a moment of becoming. It cannot be reconstructed from inputs and outputs.

Against Measurement as a Model of Mind

Measurement freezes what is fluid. It replaces continuity with coordinates.

While analysis may require such freezing, judgment does not survive it. Judgment lives in transitions and qualitative shifts that measurement smooths away.

Structural Implication

Judgment cannot be preserved by spatializing time. Systems that reduce cognition to discrete states preserve calculation while erasing judgment.

Judgment exists only where duration is preserved as duration.
