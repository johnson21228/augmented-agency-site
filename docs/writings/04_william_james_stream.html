Judgment in the Stream of Consciousness (William James)

Overview

William James rejects atomistic models of thought and replaces them with continuity. In The Principles of Psychology, consciousness is described as a stream: continuous, flowing, and irreversible.

Judgment arises within this stream. It is not an operation performed upon mental contents but a modulation of an ongoing process.

Against Discrete Mental States

James rejects the “psychologist’s fallacy” of mistaking analytic categories for lived experience. Thought does not arrive in packets.

If thought is continuous, judgment cannot be discrete.

Continuity and Meaning

Meaning arises from relations sustained across time. A present thought is intelligible only because of what precedes and follows it.

Judgment depends on this relational continuity.

Attention and Habit

Attention shapes the stream over time. What is attended to becomes salient; what is neglected fades.

Judgment expresses a history of attention and habit.

Irreversibility

The stream moves forward. Once a judgment has occurred, the conditions that produced it no longer exist in the same form.

Judgment cannot be replayed or reconstructed.

Structural Implication

Judgment does not reside in stored content. It resides in continuity.

Any system that preserves conclusions while discarding the stream preserves information while erasing judgment.
