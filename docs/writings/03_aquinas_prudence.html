Prudence as Stabilized Temporal Discernment (Aquinas)

Overview

Thomas Aquinas synthesizes Aristotle’s practical wisdom with Augustine’s interior temporality into a rigorous account of judgment as prudence (prudentia). Prudence is not calculation, instinct, or rule application. It is a stabilized capacity for right judgment in action, formed through time.

Judgment, in Aquinas’s account, depends on continuity of memory, foresight, and character.

Prudence as Practical Reason

Aquinas defines prudence as recta ratio agibilium: right reason about things to be done. Prudence concerns action in contingent situations and therefore operates under uncertainty.

Unlike speculative reason, prudence concerns particulars. Particulars are known only through experience.

Judgment cannot be abstracted from temporal engagement.

The Integral Parts of Prudence

Aquinas identifies integral parts of prudence, including: - memory, - understanding, - foresight, - circumspection, - caution.

Each is explicitly temporal. Together they form an arc from past through present toward future.

Judgment emerges only when this arc is intact.

Memory and Moral Continuity

Prudential memory is not recall of facts but truthful retention of lived experience. It is shaped by practice and habituation.

False memory corrupts judgment by distorting continuity.

Prudence is cumulative and irreversible.

Foresight and Direction

Prudence is future-oriented without being predictive. Foresight is the ability to sense where present action tends.

Judgment is directional rather than calculative. It moves along a trajectory.

Against Outcome-Based Judgment

A prudent action may fail; an imprudent one may succeed. Judgment is evaluated by discernment at the time of action, not by results.

Judgment resides in seeing and choosing under uncertainty.

Structural Implication

Judgment cannot be preserved by recording decisions alone. It requires preservation of the temporal structures through which discernment is formed.

Prudence is continuity made reliable.
